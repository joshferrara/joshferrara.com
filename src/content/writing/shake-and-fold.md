Fourteen years ago I watched a TED Talk by Joe Smith called ["How to Use a Paper Towel"](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=2FMBSblpcrc). It's about four minutes long. The premise is exactly what the title says: Smith shows that you can dry your hands with a single paper towel if you shake your hands twelve times first, then fold the towel before drying. Shake and fold. That's it.

His pitch was about scale. If every American used one less paper towel per day, we'd save 571 million pounds of paper a year. The whole point was collective: a tiny change, multiplied by millions of people, adds up to something huge.

I'll be honest, I don't know why this one stuck with me. I've watched a lot of TED Talks. Most of them I couldn't summarize a week later. But I have genuinely been shaking and folding for fourteen years. Every public restroom, every kitchen spill, every time. It's automatic now.

Here's what I'm slowly realizing, though. Joe Smith's argument was about what happens when a lot of people do a small thing. But the same logic works for just one person doing one small thing for a long time. I'm probably not personally saving the planet with my paper towels. But fourteen years of *any* tiny habit, repeated by one person, still compounds into something real. The math doesn't only work horizontally across millions of people. It works vertically across years.

What other small habits could I have started fourteen years ago that would have compounded into something meaningful in my life by now? Writing one sentence in a journal every night. Ten pushups every morning. Saving an extra fifty bucks a month into an index fund. None of these are hard. None of them require a lifestyle overhaul. But fourteen years of any of them looks dramatically different than fourteen years of nothing.

And what should I start today that I'll be glad I started by 2040? This is probably the better question. The trap with small habits is that they feel too small to matter, so you don't bother. But that's sort of the whole point. Small things stick because they're small. Big resolutions typically don't.

I guess the lesson Joe Smith was really teaching wasn't about paper towels, and it wasn't only about collective action either. It was that if you want a habit to last fourteen years, it has to be small enough that you don't have to think much about it.

Shake and fold.
