---
title: A Little Late to Country Music
date: 2026-05-17
growthStage: published
---

I grew up in the South, around country music, around people who loved country music, around pickup trucks and porches and backroads and radio stations where a steel guitar was never too far away.

And somehow, for most of my life, country music just never really clicked for me.

That sounds a little funny to admit. Not because I had some strong anti-country stance. I didn't. I was never the guy making fun of country music or rolling my eyes when someone put it on. I've always loved music too much to be that closed off about it. I play music. I listen to music constantly. I can get into pretty much any genre if the song hits right.

But country was always lower on the list.

There were exceptions, of course. My dad introduced me to certain pockets of it over the years. Dwight Yoakam. Alan Jackson. Johnny Cash. Merle Haggard. Gene Autry. Some of the older western stuff too, the kind that feels less like Nashville and more like a dusty movie reel: Roy Rogers, Marty Robbins, Tex Ritter, Bob Wills, the Sons of the Pioneers. I heard plenty of Hank Williams, Willie Nelson, Waylon Jennings, George Jones, Patsy Cline, Loretta Lynn. Enough to know the names. Enough to respect it.

But respecting something and reaching for it on a random Tuesday afternoon are two different things.

For whatever reason, country music became one of those categories I quietly filed away in my head. I didn't hate it. I just decided, somewhere along the way, that it wasn't really mine.

## A nudge from the group chat

Then last year, a friend dropped a message in our group chat.

> I'm listening to a vinyl recording of [_Golden Hour_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden_Hour_(Kacey_Musgraves_album)) from Kacey Musgraves and it's fantastic.

I noticed his message, but I didn't immediately do anything with it.

Four days later he followed up:

> Man y'all really need to listen to this album.

That second message popped up right as I was sitting down at my desk for some deep work. The timing was right so I put it on.

I don't know exactly what I expected, but I know I didn't expect to be completely knocked over by it.

The next day I told him, "It's been a long time since I went from 'never heard a single song' to 'can't get enough of this album' in a single day."

_Golden Hour_ hit me immediately, and it hit me in a way that felt almost embarrassing. This album released in 2018. How had this been sitting there for seven years? How had I missed something this good? It had the warmth and directness I associate with country music, but it also had all this space in it. It was dreamy and patient and melodic. It had songs that felt like driving at sunset. Songs that felt like sitting still for once. Songs that felt simple until you realized how carefully they were made.

I kept coming back to it.

## The floodgates open

Then I started branching out.

That was the real surprise. It wasn't just that I loved one Kacey Musgraves album. That would have been easy enough to explain away as an exception. The bigger surprise was realizing there was a whole style of country music, and country-adjacent music, that I genuinely loved. I just hadn't gone looking in the right corners.

And once you realize that, the floodgates open a little.

Suddenly I'm finding albums I should have heard years ago. Suddenly I'm paying attention to artists I would have skimmed past before. Suddenly the thing I thought was "not really my thing" is exactly the thing I want on during a quiet afternoon, or while I'm working late at night, or when I'm driving with nowhere urgent to be.

The new Ella Langley album, [_Dandelion_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dandelion_(album)), is another one that grabbed me right away. Different from _Golden Hour_, but it hits some of the same pleasure centers for me. Great songs. Great feel. A little throwback, a little modern. An album that knows exactly what it's trying to be.

And I think that's what has stuck with me most.

I spent 36 years assuming I wasn't really into country music. Then, because a friend mentioned a seven-year-old album in a group text, I found out I was wrong.

Not wrong in some dramatic, identity-shattering way. Just wrong in the ordinary way we're all wrong about ourselves sometimes.

We decide we don't like something. Or we decide we're done trying it. Or we decide that our old opinion is still accurate because it used to be accurate. And then years go by. We change, but the opinion stays in the same dusty little folder where we left it.

## Avoidance vs. lost curiosity

That's the part I keep thinking about.

Because it wasn't like I had been actively avoiding country music. I had just stopped being curious about it.

There's a difference.

Avoiding something is deliberate. You know what you're doing. You have your reasons. But losing curiosity is quieter than that. It can happen without you noticing. You give something a few tries, it doesn't land, and eventually you stop checking back in. At some point, "I haven't found the right version of this yet" turns into "this is not for me."

Sometimes that's true.

Sometimes it isn't.

In this case, it wasn't.

Now I feel a little silly, honestly. Not regretful exactly, because music tends to find you when it finds you. But silly. There were all these great songs and albums sitting right there, and I was walking around with this old conclusion in my head like it was still useful.

It makes me wonder what else I've done that with.

What other food, book, city, hobby, movie, artist, or type of music have I written off because of an earlier version of myself? What else did I try at the wrong time, in the wrong mood, through the wrong example? What else did I decide I didn't like before I had really found the door into it?

**Taste is personal, but it isn't fixed.** That's easy to forget.

We talk about taste like it's a set of permanent facts. "I like this." "I don't like that." "That's my thing." "That's not my thing." Some of that is useful. Nobody needs to live in a constant state of reconsidering every preference they've ever had. That sounds exhausting.

## Leaving the door cracked open

But there's something nice about leaving the door cracked open.

You don't have to force yourself to like things. You don't have to pretend to enjoy something just because other people do. But it's worth remembering that your taste is allowed to surprise you. You are allowed to come around. You are allowed to find the right album 36 years in.

Especially when the album is that good.

So I guess this is my small, slightly late confession: I like country music now.

Maybe I always liked more of it than I realized. Maybe I needed the right record. Maybe I needed a friend to be persistent enough to recommend the same album twice in four days. Maybe I needed to be a little older, a little more patient, a little more willing to hear what was actually there instead of what I assumed would be there.

Whatever the reason, I'm glad it happened.

And if there's a larger lesson in it, it's probably this: don't let your old opinions do all your listening for you.

They might be out of date.
