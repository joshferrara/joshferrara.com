---
title: Tire Pressure Sensors
date: 2026-04-27
growthStage: published
---

Years ago, after a few years of driving a handed down car from my parents, I got my first newish car. Among the other exciting features for a 19-year-old, it had tire pressure sensors. A few months in, I started getting alerts. Tire pressure low. Tire pressure high. It seemed like I was seeing a tire pressure alert popping up every other month.

I was convinced something was wrong. New car, already broken. I figured I'd gotten a lemon, or there was some defect I'd have to fight a dealership about.

So I called my dad. Then I took it to a mechanic. Both of them told me the same thing: tire pressure fluctuates. It's normal, especially in Louisiana, where we'd swing from 90º afternoons to 40º mornings in the same week. Cold air contracts, warm air expands, and the pressure changes accordingly. Embarrassingly basic science. Nothing was wrong with the car. Nothing was even really wrong with the tires.

The thing that was actually new wasn't the problem. It was the visibility.

My old car had the same fluctuation happening the whole time I owned it. I just had no way to know, because I wasn't manually checking. The new sensor wasn't causing the issue, it was reporting one that had likely always been there. And once I understood that, I stopped being annoyed at the car and started actually checking my tires more often, which, honestly, I should have been doing all along.

I've thought about this on and off through the years because the same pattern seems to show up everywhere in life.

A new friend points out a bad habit you didn't realize you had developed. A new job expects more discipline than your last one did. A new relationship surfaces something about how you handle conflict. And the easy move, the very human move, is to blame the new thing. They're too critical. The job is too demanding. This person is making me feel some feelings I didn't feel before.

But most of the time the new thing is just the sensor. The pressure was already off. You just didn't have anything telling you.

That habit was already there. The lack of discipline was already there. The way you handle conflict was already there. None of it started when the new person or new situation showed up. They're just the first thing that happened to give you some visibility into it.

In hindsight, I think the move is to resist the urge to shoot the messenger. When something new comes into your life and starts surfacing friction, it's worth asking whether the friction is actually new, or whether you're just finally noticing it. Sometimes the answer is yes, this person or situation really is the problem. But pretty often, the answer is that they've handed you a useful piece of information about yourself, and what you do with it is up to you.

Don't get mad at the tire pressure sensor. Check the tires.
