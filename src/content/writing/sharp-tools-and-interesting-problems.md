I heard this idea years ago. I've never been able to find a clean source for it, which makes me think it's either well-worn folk wisdom or a mashup of a few things I heard and started repeating as one. Either way, it's the clearest lens I have for how to hire and keep skilled craftspeople. Designers, engineers, editors, videographers, anyone whose work depends on a craft and not just on throughput or talking the right people into things.

The idea: craftspeople need sharp tools and interesting problems. If you want to keep them, you need both.

## Sharp tools

Sharp tools means giving people the things they actually need to do the job well. For an engineer, that's a fast machine, a paid seat of the IDE they prefer, CI that works, the license for the database tool, decent internet, and the authority to adopt a new tool without a six-week procurement fight. For a designer, it's font licenses, a Figma seat that isn't rationed, stock resources, a good monitor, and permission to buy the plugin without a PO. For a video team, it's the right cameras, lenses, cards, and a workflow that doesn't collapse under 4K footage.

Sharp tools aren't just software and hardware. The environment counts too. A quiet place to focus. An office next to the teammates you actually work with. Or, often the sharpest tool of all, permission to work from home when the work needs it. Time counts too. Enough of it, unfragmented, to do the work at the standard the craftsperson holds themselves to.

This isn't about splurging. Nobody needs a $6,000 chair. It's about removing the small frictions that, stacked up, tell a craftsperson "we don't really care if you do this well." When you skimp on tools, that's the message you're sending, whether you mean to or not. They hear it.

## Interesting problems

The second half is interesting problems. Craftspeople want work that stretches them. The big goal. The thing they're not sure they can pull off. The project they'd actually want to talk about at a dinner or put in a portfolio. Work that advances a career, earns some respect in the field, or just scratches the itch that made them pick this craft in the first place.

The opposite of interesting isn't "hard." It's repetitive, safe, and predictable. Shipping variation #47 of the same client project. Building the same marketing site again. Cutting the same promo reel for the same kind of customer. Some of that work is unavoidable and often pays the bills. But if it's *all* they get, skilled people leave eventually, even when every other condition is good.

Interesting problems can come from lots of places. A bigger technical challenge. A new domain. A client doing something genuinely new. An internal R&D bet. A pro bono project with real stakes. The specifics matter less than the pattern: you are regularly putting meaningful work in front of your people.

## The two-by-two

A simple grid is useful here, as long as you take it with a grain of salt. Real workplaces drift around these boxes, and "sharp" and "interesting" are relative to the person. Still:

|                        |**Sharp tools**                |**Dull tools**                 |
|------------------------|-------------------------------|-------------------------------|
|**Interesting problems**|Easy to attract, easy to retain|Easy to attract, hard to retain|
|**Boring problems**     |Easy to attract, hard to retain|Hard to attract, hard to retain|

**Sharp tools, interesting problems.** This is where the best craftspeople gather and stay. The work challenges them and they have what they need to meet it. You don't need anything else fancy; you just need both of these.

**Sharp tools, boring problems.** The well-resourced shop with no ambition. Easy sell on the way in: look at our gear, our budget, our headcount, our benefits. People show up excited. Then, six to eighteen months later, the craftsperson notices that nothing they're doing is actually *interesting*. The tools are great, but the work is the same shape every quarter. They leave for something smaller and more alive, sometimes taking a pay cut to do it.

**Interesting problems, dull tools.** The mission-driven startup. The idealistic nonprofit. The scrappy studio doing real work on underpowered laptops. They have no trouble attracting talent. People *want* in. But the friction wears on you. You're doing great work with one hand tied behind your back, and eventually you decide you'd like to do great work *without* the handicap. You leave for somewhere with both.

**Dull tools, boring problems.** There's no hiring story here for craftspeople. You fill seats with people who aren't really craftspeople, or with craftspeople who are stuck and watching the door. This quadrant can run on inertia for a surprisingly long time. It doesn't retain anyone whose craft is central to who they are.

## Which half matters more

If pushed, I'd put interesting problems slightly ahead. Craftspeople will tolerate surprisingly bad tools for a while if the work is genuinely exciting. I've seen it happen. They almost never tolerate boring work, no matter how nice the setup.

But that's a narrow reading. You don't actually get to pick one. Solve only for tools and you build a comfortable place where ambitious people don't stay. Solve only for problems and you build an exciting place where frustrated people burn out. Both halves are the job.

## How to use this

If you run a team, audit yourself honestly on both. Not "do we *say* we care about craft" but what a random Tuesday actually looks like for your best senior IC. Are they fighting their tools? Are they working on something they'd be proud to put in their portfolio? If either answer is no, that's the work.

And if you're on the other side of the table, evaluating a place to work, ask about both. It's easy to be dazzled by one and miss the other. The gear tour is seductive. So is the mission pitch. Ask what the last genuinely hard problem was that the team took on. Ask what someone does when they need a new tool or a license. The answers usually tell you which box you're walking into.
